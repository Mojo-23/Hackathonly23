# Document 2 — Master Implementation Brief
### For: design engineer / product team implementing on Hackathonly Jordan
### Self-sufficient — implementable without reading Document 1

**One-line context:** TAIKAI (reference hackathon platform) was analyzed across 9 screenshots (landing, organizations ×2 variants, hackathons listing, blog). It is a maximalist, violet/Web3-coded, high-density B2B/B2C hybrid product. Its **structural mechanisms** (density-alternation rhythm, module-rail product previews, fragment-collage richness, card content-type variety, per-instance visual differentiation) are strong and transferable. Its **entire surface language** (unrationed purple, gradient text, illustrated/NFT/Web3 imagery, dark glowing heroes) is explicitly incompatible with Hackathonly's Sandstone Editorial system and must not be copied.

**MY_PRODUCT brand constraints (restated in full, binding on every direction below):**
- Pure white ground (`#FFFFFF`), ink typography (`#191714`), one Petra-clay accent (`#A03D21`) rationed to ≤3 uses per screen (primary action, active nav, at most one accent detail).
- Warm-stone neutrals for borders/secondary surfaces. Exactly two shadows, five radii, closed 4px spacing scale.
- No gradients (except flat system-generated cover fields), no glassmorphism, no illustration/mascots, no purple/Web3 aesthetic, no dark-mode-first design. Exactly **one** sanctioned dark surface, used only as a closing CTA band, never a hero.
- Privacy-first messaging is structural, not decorative: no public marketplace, no public people browsing, no public contact reveal, no AI winner selection, no public negative scoring, no sponsor access without opt-in.
- Jordanian/university-first context; "serious builder culture" — calm, protective, institutional-grade, never gamified, never crypto-coded, never flags/Petra-silhouette/tourism-kitsch decoration.
- Existing D19 motion system (`src/lib/motion/`, `src/components/motion/`) is the only motion vocabulary — reuse it, do not parallel it.
- **No fabricated data.** No invented logos, testimonials, statistics, or competitor comparisons. Every number on a public page must trace to real mock data (`src/lib/mock-data.ts`) or be honestly omitted.

Every direction below has been checked against this list; none require an exception.

> **Status: reference/implementation brief, executed and adopted.** The directions in this document were implemented across the public experience (landing, `/organizers`, `/participants`, `/blog`, `/events`, shared header/footer), and that implementation is now the **official Hackathonly public design** — not an experiment, prototype, or proposed direction. See `handoff/CLAUDE_IMPLEMENTATION_SUMMARY.md` for the current, authoritative build record. This brief remains useful as the reasoning trail for *why* each structural choice was made; it is not itself the live spec — the shipped code and `docs/DESIGN_SYSTEM.md`/`docs/MOTION_SYSTEM.md` are.

> **Revision note (binding human decision, post-publication):** this brief originally recommended reverting event-cover and avatar photography to a fully typographic/geometric system, on the grounds that the existing implementation used runtime-hotlinked external images. The human has since made an explicit, binding correction: **the photography direction itself is approved and must be kept — the hotlinking method was the only problem.** Directions 6 and 7 below, the affected Gap Analysis rows, and Priority Action Plan item 1 have all been rewritten accordingly. Photography must be downloaded, stored locally under `public/images/`, optimized, consistently cropped, curated, and color-graded to Sandstone Editorial — never hotlinked, never generic corporate stock. A typographic/geometric fallback remains available only for cases with no approved image.

## PHASE 5 — MY_PRODUCT Translation (13 directions)

**1. Hero / opening direction.** Keep Hackathonly's current pattern (centered eyebrow → two-line ink headline → one-sentence quiet subhead → one dominant primary CTA + one quiet text-link secondary CTA → large bordered product-preview panel below). Borrowed from TAIKAI: the preview's *size and dominance* relative to the text block, and the "browser-chrome-equivalent" framing device (Hackathonly's existing bordered panel + header bar + status pill already serves this role — keep it, don't add literal browser dots). Rejected from TAIKAI: gradient headline text, two-equal-CTA forced choice, illustrated cover imagery inside the preview.

**2. Primary content-card direction (event cards).** Keep the existing cover-photo-band + theme badge + bracket motif + typographic event-mark anatomy. Borrowed from TAIKAI: card content-type variety is not needed here (event cards should stay anatomy-consistent — this is a *listing* context, consistency aids scanning, unlike TAIKAI's *feature* grid which benefits from variety) — the one TAIKAI idea worth adopting is **per-instance visual differentiation for recurring event series** (Phase 4A-7 in Document 1): if Hackathonly ever lists multiple instances of one recurring event (e.g. a university's annual hackathon across years, or a multi-city series), differentiate covers by a real, non-nationalistic attribute (host university mark, city name treatment) rather than letting near-duplicate cards read as indistinguishable.

**3. Primary "seller"/organizer audience page direction (`/organizers`).** Keep the current structure (hero → credibility strip → lifecycle clusters → product-surface grid → privacy panel → audience paragraph → closer). Borrowed from TAIKAI: the module-rail + big-metric-tiles + operational-widget hero preview anatomy (already implemented, validated by this research as correct) and the fragment-collage principle for the product-surface grid (already partially present — lean further into content-type variety per card, e.g. one tile shows a counter, one shows a status line, one shows a mini-checklist, rather than seven identical metric tiles). Rejected: benchmark/competitor comparison table (no honest dataset exists yet — do not fabricate), client logo wall (no real logos yet — do not fabricate or imply).

**4. Primary "user"/participant audience page direction (`/` landing).** Keep current structure. Borrowed from TAIKAI: the density-alternation rhythm (already partially adopted this session via the "quiet break" section) — extend it as a standing rule for any future section added to this page. Rejected: the "find teammates" literal face-browsing implication (Document 1, Screenshot 2) — Hackathonly's own privacy-first positioning forbids implying an open people-directory; keep team-formation messaging scoped to "your event's opted-in pool," never a general browse.

**5. Product/dashboard-preview direction.** Both existing hero previews (`/` and `/organizers`) already implement the correct pattern (bordered panel, header bar, status pill, module content). Standing rule going forward: every future product preview must (a) show real mock-data-derived numbers, never placeholder zeros dressed up as real, (b) cap visible density at "enough to feel real, never enough to feel like a raw admin table" (3–5 metrics, one operational widget, never a full data table), (c) never use TAIKAI's fragment-scatter *layout* asymmetry — Hackathonly's previews stay grid-disciplined even when applying the fragment-variety *content* idea.

**6. Imagery direction — CORRECTED, binding.** Photography is approved for event covers and stays part of Hackathonly's identity. No illustration, no AI-generated art, no gradient/neon treatment, and — separately — **no runtime hotlinking to any external image host**. Every image used must be: (a) downloaded and committed as a local asset under `public/images/` (never fetched from a third-party CDN at runtime), (b) optimized and served via `next/image` or the project's existing local-asset pattern, (c) consistently cropped to a fixed aspect ratio across every card, (d) curated and color-graded to sit inside Sandstone Editorial's calm register (warm-neutral, not saturated, not glossy-corporate), and (e) documented in a source/license manifest. Curation standard: reject generic corporate stock photography, handshakes, suits, staged fake offices, and tourism clichés; prefer authentic young builders, university teams, laptops, workshops, whiteboards, and real coding environments — imagery that could plausibly be from an actual Jordanian university hackathon, even before real photography exists. A typographic/geometric fallback (the system-generated cover treatment already built) remains available and should be used only when no approved image exists for a given card — it is a fallback, not the default.

**7. Avatar / identity-representation direction — CORRECTED, binding.** Realistic participant avatars are approved and should be kept for team-formation/preview contexts (e.g. the landing page's "Team formation queue"). The requirement is sourcing, not removal: avatars must be downloaded and stored locally under `public/images/` (never hotlinked to a third-party avatar service at runtime), consistently cropped (same aspect ratio, same framing convention across every avatar), and curated for a credible, professional-but-human portrait style — not glamour photography, not stock-agency headshots, not cartoon/illustrated, not visibly AI-generated. Where no approved local avatar image exists for a given mock person, fall back to the typographic/geometric identity chip (initials, circular, tone-rotated) rather than leaving a broken image or reaching for a hotlinked placeholder. The brand system (tokens, radius, border treatment) remains dominant — the photo supports the card, it does not replace the card's Sandstone Editorial framing (border, radius, spacing stay exactly as specified elsewhere in this system).

**8. Motion direction.** No new motion system, no new library. Reuse exactly what exists: `MotionReveal`, `MotionSection`, `MotionStagger`/`MotionStaggerItem`, `MotionCard`, `MotionCounter`, `FloatingElement`, `useCursorParallax`, `useReducedMotionSafe`, and the token file `src/lib/motion/tokens.ts`. Borrowed from TAIKAI (inferred only): count-up on metric tiles (already implemented via `MotionCounter`), a status-pulse on "Live" indicators (already implemented). Nothing observed in TAIKAI's static evidence implies motion beyond what Hackathonly already has — no new motion capability is needed to close this gap.

**9. Mobile direction.** The riskiest single element observed in TAIKAI's product-preview pattern is the module-rail sidebar — Hackathonly has already solved this correctly (`hidden ... lg:flex`, single-column stack below `lg`) on both existing hero previews. Standing rule: any new product-preview surface must hide its module rail (or equivalent wide-format element) below `lg` and verify no horizontal overflow at 375px, exactly as the existing previews already do.

**10. CTA hierarchy.** One dominant primary CTA per hero (already corrected this session), quiet text-link secondary in heroes, bordered-button pairs reserved for true "two equally valid next steps" moments (final closers), never for hero-level primary/secondary pairs. This is already fully implemented — no further change required, only discipline to maintain it on future pages.

**11. Section rhythm.** Standing rule for all future public pages: never stack more than two visually-dense sections without a low-density rest (a plain paragraph, a divided list, a FAQ-style accordion, or a logo-wall-equivalent). Both existing public pages already comply after this session's polish pass; apply the same rule to any future page (e.g. a future `/pricing` or `/about`).

**12. Visual-density strategy.** Per-section target: state the section's single idea in the eyebrow+heading, support it with one supporting visual (card grid, preview panel, or divided list — never more than one dense device per section), and never let a single section carry two competing dense elements (e.g. a data table *and* a card grid in the same section).

**13. The one emotional target + three mechanisms.** **Target: "quiet, earned confidence" — the feeling of a serious institution that has already solved the hard problems (privacy, operations, credibility) so you don't have to worry about them.** This is the calm-authority inverse of TAIKAI's "exciting + legitimate" hero emotion (Document 1, Phase 2M) — same underlying goal (trust + desire to join), opposite emotional route (restraint instead of saturation). Three mechanisms chosen to deliver it, all already partially or fully implemented: (1) the audited/structural privacy language (contact reveal mechanics, consent versioning) stated as concrete mechanism, never as a vague promise — this is Hackathonly's equivalent of TAIKAI's benchmark table: authority through specificity, not adjectives; (2) restrained, single-accent-color typographic hierarchy (the clay rationing law) doing the work TAIKAI's saturated color does, at a fraction of the visual noise; (3) the density-alternation rhythm, borrowed structurally from TAIKAI but executed in Sandstone Editorial's quieter register, so confidence reads as unhurried rather than as "nothing here."

## Final Design Direction Summary (one page, quotable)

**Hackathonly is what TAIKAI's own design team would build if TAIKAI's category were "privacy-first, institutional, Jordanian" instead of "Web3, global, hustle-coded."**

Steal TAIKAI's structure — the density-alternation rhythm that keeps a long two-audience page from exhausting the visitor, the module-rail-plus-metrics product-preview language that proves the product is real without showing a raw admin panel, the card-content-type variety that defeats the identical-icon-wall trap, the discipline of never running more than two dense sections back to back. Reject everything TAIKAI uses paint to say — the wallpapered violet, the gradient headlines, the illustrated Web3 covers, the NFT badges, the dark glowing hero. Where TAIKAI reaches for saturation to produce excitement, Hackathonly reaches for restraint to produce trust — same underlying goal (a stranger decides this is real and worth joining), opposite mechanism, because the audiences and the promise are different: TAIKAI is selling adrenaline to a crypto-native crowd; Hackathonly is selling calm, audited, earned confidence to students and organizers who've been burned by WhatsApp chaos and spreadsheet chaos before.

The single most important number in this whole brief is **≤3** — the clay-color rationing law. Everything TAIKAI gets wrong (Document 1, Phase 3: Color precision, 4/10 — its lowest score) is a direct consequence of not having that number. Everything Hackathonly should protect, as it grows, is exactly that discipline.
